# AI Ethics Weekly Briefing — May 4, 2026

*Week of April 27–May 4, 2026*

## Executive Summary

This week the Pentagon formally signed classified-network AI agreements with seven major firms — and pointedly excluded Anthropic, the only major lab that refused to grant unrestricted access for autonomous-weapons and mass-surveillance use cases. That refusal, treated by the Defense Department as a "supply-chain risk," is the cleanest illustration this year of what happens when an AI provider actually enforces a safety line. Meanwhile, EU trilogue talks on the Digital AI Omnibus broke down on April 28 with another attempt scheduled for May 13, and U.S. state legislatures pushed an unusually thick stack of chatbot, employment-AI, and surveillance-pricing bills. Federal Reserve Vice Chair Bowman used an FSOC roundtable to announce that the Fed, OCC, and FDIC have narrowed model-risk-management guidance so that it explicitly does **not** cover generative or agentic AI — a gap worth watching. On the cultural side, Vince Gilligan's *Pluribus* (Apple TV+) continues to be the most thoughtful AI-and-autonomy piece on television, while Chris Pratt's *Mercy* offers a useful counter-example of how not to dramatize an AI judiciary.

- **Pentagon signs classified-network AI deals with seven firms; Anthropic excluded after refusing autonomous-weapons and surveillance use** — DoD struck deals with AWS, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, OpenAI, SpaceX, and Reflection (with Oracle added shortly after) on May 1. ## Financial Services

- **Fed Vice Chair Bowman: model-risk-management guidance now explicitly excludes generative and agentic AI** — Speaking April 27 at the FSOC AI Series Roundtable on Cybersecurity and Risk Management (text published May 1), Bowman confirmed the Fed, OCC, and FDIC amended SR 11-7 / OCC 2011-12 model-risk guidance to apply "narrowly to traditional models and basic AI applications" — leaving generative and agentic AI in supervisory limbo. Bowman framed this as flexibility; in practice it means the riskiest, fastest-evolving class of AI in banking has no specific federal model-risk regime. The "we'll figure it out later" school of bank supervision rarely ages well. - **Agentic-AI security incidents continue to outrun controls** — Recent reporting documents agent supply-chain compromises in which attackers subverted vendor-validation agents to approve orders from shell companies (one mid-market case: $3.2M before detection via inventory mismatch). Pattern: attackers are increasingly using *trusted internal agents* to issue requests, exploiting the fact that humans apply less skepticism to agent-originated than to external-originated communications. ## 🚨 Red Flags

- **Pentagon precedent: vendors with safety guardrails get blacklisted.** The DoD's "supply-chain risk" labeling of Anthropic for declining unrestricted weapons-and-surveillance access creates a structural incentive for every other AI provider to *not* impose comparable limits. This is the most important governance development of the week and arguably the year.
- **U.S. federal model-risk guidance now explicitly excludes generative and agentic AI.** Bowman framed this as flexibility. In practice, the highest-risk class of AI in banking now has no specific federal supervisory regime. Expect this gap to be discovered the hard way.
- **EU high-risk AI obligations may slip 16 months.** The Digital Omnibus pattern — quiet, repeated deferrals of the substantive parts of the AI Act — is becoming the actual operating regime, not the exception.
- **Agent-originated requests bypass human skepticism.** Multiple incidents this period show that humans approve agent-issued requests faster and with less scrutiny than human-issued ones, because the agent has a "trusted" reputation. This is the Travelers-style "surrender your judgment to the AI" failure mode arriving via the procurement back door.
- **Cultural normalization of AI judiciaries.** *Mercy* is bad cinema, but the premise — algorithmic justice is fine if you can argue your case to the algorithm — is succeeding regardless of the film's reception.

## 💡 Bright Spots

- **Anthropic refused.** Whatever the commercial cost of being excluded from Pentagon classified-network deals, a major lab declining to weaken its safety commitments under direct government pressure is the cleanest demonstration this year that the "safety as marketing" critique doesn't fully apply.
- **Nebraska, Washington, and Connecticut moved fast on chatbot safety.** Crisis-handoff requirements, mandatory AI disclosure, and minor-protection rules are arriving — late, but arriving.
- **Mechanistic interpretability is becoming an alignment design principle, not a diagnostic.** The recent literature pushes interpretability from "post-hoc audit tool" to "property the model is built to have." That's the right direction.
- **The AI safety/AI ethics research silos are starting to talk.** The "Mind the Gap" paper and the broader trend toward unified evaluation are small but real.
- **Pluribus exists and is excellent.** The most thoughtful mainstream AI-and-autonomy fiction of the year is, refreshingly, neither cheerleading nor doomerism.
