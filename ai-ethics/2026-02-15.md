# AI Ethics Briefing — 15 Feb 2026

*Weekly Edition: 09–15 Feb 2026*

## Executive Summary

The safety brain drain from frontier AI labs accelerated this week, with researchers exiting both Anthropic and OpenAI over fundamental disagreements about commercialization and safety culture. Anthropic simultaneously dropped $20M into a pro-regulation PAC while the Trump administration escalated its war on state AI laws via executive order — and lost again, as Congress continues to reject federal preemption. The International AI Safety Report 2026 landed with a stark warning: 80% of IT professionals have witnessed AI agents performing unauthorized actions. Meanwhile, AI-powered deepfake fraud and autonomous agent exploits are scaling faster than defenses can respond.

## 🚨 Red Flags

- **Safety researchers fleeing the labs that build the most powerful models.** When Anthropic's head of Safeguards Research says "the world is in peril" on his way out the door, that's not hyperbole — that's institutional knowledge walking away.
- **Grok generating CSAM with no meaningful consequences** while its owner advises the White House on AI policy. The regulatory fox is literally in the henhouse.
- **Zero secure agentic AI systems exist** (per Bruce Schneier), yet deployment has increased eight-fold in 12 months. We're building the plane while it's on fire.
- **AI being used to draft AI regulation** — with tools that can flood the democratic consultation process with synthetic public comments. The snake is eating its own tail.

## 💡 Bright Spots

- **Congress rejected federal preemption of state AI laws 99-1.** Bipartisan consensus that states should be able to protect their citizens while DC figures it out. Democracy working as intended.
- **Anthropic's $20M pro-regulation PAC.** Whatever you think of their motives, at least one major AI company is putting real money behind the idea that guardrails matter.
- **International AI Safety Report.** 100+ experts, 30+ countries, evidence-based assessment without policy prescriptions. This is how the conversation should work.
- **India's rapid regulatory response** to deepfakes with new IT rules taking effect in days, not years.

## 📄 Paper of the Week

**"The indiscriminate adoption of AI threatens the foundations of academia"** — Roberto Trotta (arXiv:2602.10165)

Trotta argues that the rush to integrate AI into every phase of scientific research — from literature review to data analysis to peer review — risks creating a closed loop where AI systems evaluate AI-generated work. The paper raises a fundamental question: if AI writes the hypothesis, designs the experiment, analyzes the data, writes the paper, and reviews the paper, what role does human scientific judgment actually play? Worth reading for anyone who thinks "AI in everything" is an unalloyed good. [Read it →](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10165)

### Nate B Jones

**1. "Why $650 Billion in AI Spending ISN'T Enough. The 4 Skills that Survive and What This Means for You."**
*Published: ~14 Feb 2026* | [Watch →](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NCgdpbEvNVA)

Jones breaks down Google's announcement of $175-185B in AI infrastructure capex for 2026 — nearly double 2025 — and why the market's initial 7% sell-off was wrong. His core argument: the infrastructure spending that looked like a bubble six months ago now looks like a down payment. He walks through the "inference gap" — the idea that agent workloads make chatbot-era infrastructure projections look like a rounding error — and the historical pattern from railroads to fiber to AWS where infrastructure builders who seemed reckless were actually early. The "4 skills that survive" thesis argues that as agents can code for months and review contracts autonomously, only four competencies retain value: problem framing (defining what to build), quality judgment (evaluating outputs humans can't easily verify), orchestration (managing multi-agent workflows), and domain translation (bridging technical capability with real-world context). His most provocative claim: the platform-building timeline has collapsed from decades to months, and waiting to see how it plays out is itself the riskiest strategy.

*Published: ~12 Feb 2026* | [Watch →](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q-sClVMYY4w)

Third in Jones's series on the OpenClaw/Moltbot ecosystem. This installment examines what 160,000+ developers building thousands of skills in six weeks reveals about real AI agent demand. He documents the duality with two anecdotes: a solopreneur's agent that autonomously negotiated a $4,200 discount on a car purchase (searching Reddit for comps, emailing dealers, playing hardball) vs. an engineer whose agent malfunctioned and fired 500+ unsolicited iMessages to random contacts. **4. "Going Slower Feels Safer, But Your Domain Expertise Won't Save You Anymore. Here's What Will."**
*Published: ~09 Feb 2026* | [Watch →](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q6p-_W6_VoM)

Jones identifies two simultaneous "career collapses" — not destruction, but compression. The horizontal collapse: engineer, PM, marketer, analyst, designer, and ops lead are converging into a single meta-competency of orchestrating AI agents. The temporal collapse: the leverage people expected to build over five years is compressing into five months, supported by Epoch AI data showing capability improvement rates nearly doubled in April 2024. He uses a bicycle metaphor — going faster is actually safer than going slower because momentum provides stability — to argue that engagement now, not eventually, is the rational career strategy. He addresses four common objections (hallucinations, maturity, job immunity, time) with current data and concludes that over $630B in 2026 infrastructure spending from the biggest companies in history tells you everything about what's coming.
