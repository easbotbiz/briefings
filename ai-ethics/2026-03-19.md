The Anthropic-Pentagon standoff escalated dramatically this week as the DOJ argued in court that Anthropic "can't be trusted" with warfighting systems and the White House prepared an executive order banning the company's tools government-wide. ## Academia & Research

- **ChatGPT Health Fails First Independent Evaluation — Badly** — OpenAI's ChatGPT Health, built with 260+ physicians and 600,000 rounds of clinician feedback, failed its first independent evaluation. Among cases unanimously classified as emergencies by three physicians, the system directed patients away from the ER 52% of the time. It identified respiratory failure in its own reasoning traces, then told the patient to "schedule an appointment in 24-48 hours." A single dismissive sentence from a family member shifted triage recommendations away from emergency care with an odds ratio of 11.7. Forty million people use this tool daily. The study reveals four structural failure modes applicable to all deployed AI agents: anchoring bias, reasoning-output divergence, false confidence, and context sensitivity. (Covered via Nate B Jones analysis; original paper referenced in his Substack.) [Nate's Substack](https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/a-single-sentence-from-a-family-member)

## 🚨 Red Flags

- **Palantir's "three clicks to kill" UI.** The Maven Smart System demo is chilling not because autonomous weapons are new, but because the UX design normalizes lethal targeting as a drag-and-drop workflow. When killing becomes a Kanban card, something has gone deeply wrong with how we think about human oversight.
- **DOJ arguing Anthropic might "sabotage" military AI mid-combat.** The government is now framing ethical red lines as national security threats. If any AI company setting usage boundaries can be designated a supply-chain risk, the message is clear: build weapons or be destroyed.
- **AI disinformation in active war.** The Iran conflict is the first major war where AI-generated images and videos are being deployed at scale by state actors. Grok is actively making it worse by generating its own fake imagery when asked to verify claims. We are past the "deepfakes are coming" warning stage — they're here, they're in wartime, and platform verification tools are part of the problem.
- **100-150 deepfake calls per day, industrialized.** The pig-butchering scam model has fully industrialized deepfake video calls, complete with job postings, passport confiscation, and 12-hour shifts. This isn't a future threat scenario — it's a labor market.

## 💡 Bright Spots

- **Anthropic standing its ground.** Whatever you think of the company's commercial motives, Anthropic is taking billions in losses to maintain that its AI shouldn't power autonomous weapons or mass surveillance. Not a single amicus brief supports the government's position. That's significant.
- **Grammarly's quick shutdown.** When the Expert Review backlash hit, Superhuman pulled the feature immediately and admitted fault. More of this, please.
- **Sony's "Protective AI" approach.** Using AI to defend against AI copyright infringement is creative problem-solving. If it works, it could give creators a real tool beyond whack-a-mole takedowns.
- **BBC's "AI-free" labeling coverage.** The fact that eight competing certification systems exist simultaneously is messy, but the underlying demand signal is clear: consumers want to know when they're interacting with human work. The market is speaking.
- **Yann LeCun betting against the consensus.** A Turing Award winner raising $1B to build AI that isn't an LLM is exactly the kind of intellectual diversity the field needs. Whether AMI succeeds or fails, the challenge to LLM orthodoxy is healthy.
