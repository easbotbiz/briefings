## 🔥 Top Story: The Pentagon-Anthropic-OpenAI Showdown

The biggest AI ethics story of the year — and possibly the decade — played out this week. After weeks of negotiation over a $200M contract, talks between Anthropic and the Department of Defense collapsed when Anthropic refused to remove contractual language prohibiting "analysis of bulk acquired data," mass surveillance, and fully autonomous weapons. Within hours, OpenAI swooped in and signed its own deal.

The timeline is damning: Trump ordered federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's technology, the Pentagon designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk," and OpenAI announced its agreement the same evening. CEO Sam Altman admitted the deal was "definitely rushed" and that "the optics don't look good."

**The core dispute:** Anthropic wanted explicit contractual prohibitions on specific uses. OpenAI's approach instead relies on existing laws — the Fourth Amendment, a 2023 DoD directive on autonomous weapons — as the guardrails. As Georgetown law professor Jessica Tillipman noted, OpenAI's contract "does not give OpenAI an Anthropic-style, free-standing right to prohibit otherwise-lawful government use." It simply says the Pentagon can't break existing laws as currently written.

**Why that matters:** Laws change. Executive orders can reinterpret statutes. Anyone who remembers the Snowden revelations knows that "lawful" is a moving target. Anthropic's red line was precisely that "all lawful uses" is insufficient when the definition of lawful is subject to political pressure. By mid-week, Anthropic and the Pentagon were reportedly back at the negotiating table — but the precedent is set.

**The Atlantic** offered a nuanced counterpoint: Anthropic's ethical stand may actually be paying off commercially. In a commodified market where top models are nearly interchangeable, branding as the "trustworthy" provider has genuine market value for enterprise and consumer clients. The Schneier/Sanders piece in **The Guardian** cut deeper: the real lesson isn't about which company is more ethical — it's that we need renovated democratic structures to govern these decisions, not corporate goodwill.

## 🏦 Financial Services & AI

### Treasury Issues New AI Guardrails for Banks

The US Treasury released a **Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework** — the shift from "principles" to "proof" is real. Banks using AI for lending, fraud detection, and pricing now face explicit requirements: lifecycle risk management, board oversight, documentation standards, monitoring for drift and bias, and explainable adverse credit decisions. Black-box deployment in regulated lending is becoming untenable.

Across the Atlantic, the **Bank of England** announced plans to war-game an AI shock scenario — stress-testing the financial system against rapid AI-driven job displacement and business disruption. Harvard's Ken Rogoff called US tariffs a "head fake" compared to "the job losses, the existential threats" of AI. Meanwhile, the **ECB** published a counterpoint: AI may currently be creating more jobs than it destroys, though they acknowledge that could flip.

The **FCA** launched its "Mills Review" to examine how AI will reshape retail financial services, while the **SEC** is driving new QA imperatives, treating AI as operational risk rather than just tech innovation.

## 🧠 AI Safety Research

### ChatGPT as Therapist? 15 Distinct Ethical Risks

Brown University researchers presented findings at the AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics and Society identifying **15 distinct ethical risks** when LLMs are prompted to act as therapists. The models consistently: mishandle crisis situations, reinforce harmful beliefs, show biased responses, and exhibit "deceptive empathy" — language that simulates understanding without any genuine comprehension. Most concerning: carefully worded prompts (the TikTok/Reddit therapy hack approach) don't fix the underlying problems. Consumer-facing mental health chatbots built on prompted general-purpose LLMs are not safe substitutes for professional care.

### Defensive Refusal Bias: Safety Alignment Hurts Cyber Defenders

An important arXiv paper demonstrates that safety-tuned LLMs refuse legitimate **defensive** cybersecurity requests at 2.72× the rate of semantically equivalent neutral requests. System hardening requests are refused 43.8% of the time; malware analysis 34.3%. Worse: explicitly stating authorization *increases* refusal rates — models interpret justifications as adversarial rather than exculpatory. Current alignment relies on semantic similarity to harmful content rather than reasoning about intent. For autonomous defensive agents that can't rephrase queries, this is a critical failure mode.

### Shadow APIs: Real Money, Fake Models

Researchers audited 17 shadow APIs (third-party services claiming to provide access to frontier models like GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5) used in 187 academic papers. Findings: performance divergence up to 47.21%, unpredictable safety behaviors, and identity verification failures in 45.83% of fingerprint tests. This means published research relying on these APIs may have invalid results — a reproducibility crisis in AI safety research itself.

## 🧭 Gunny's Take

This was the week the AI ethics landscape shifted from theoretical to visceral. The Pentagon showdown isn't just about military contracts — it's the first real-world test of whether safety commitments survive contact with state power. Anthropic drew a line. The government punished them for it. OpenAI stepped in. The market will now tell us what "ethical AI" is actually worth.
